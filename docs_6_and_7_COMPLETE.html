Each project evaluated on 5 criteria, max 5 points each.



CRITERION #1: INNOVATIVE (5 POINTS)


Official Definition:

"Be bold and bring products and solutions never seen before"


Scoring Rubric:


5 POINTS - BREAKTHROUGH INNOVATION:
  • Fundamentally new approach never seen in luxury fragrance
    • Combines multiple innovations in unprecedented way
      • "Culture de l'Écart" embodiment - true twist/disruption
        • Experts would recognize as avant-garde
          • Patent-worthy or proprietary technology involved

Examples:
  • Osmobloom™ extraction (capturing previously impossible scents)
    • YSL Scent-Sation (neuroscience + AI olfactory profiling)
      • HAPTA (motion-stabilizing beauty device)

Questions to Ask:
  • Has this EVER been done before in fragrance category?
    • Would experts describe this as "revolutionary"?
      • Does it push boundaries in unexpected direction?
        • Could it create a new subcategory?


4 POINTS - HIGHLY INNOVATIVE:
  • Significant innovation with meaningful novelty
    • Adapts breakthrough from another industry to fragrance
      • Multiple innovative elements combined
        • Clear differentiation from current offerings

Examples:
  • First multi-brand refill fountain network
    • AR + AI + generative algorithms for beauty (YSL Hyper Look)
      • Lab-on-chip for skin assessment (Lancôme Cell BioPrint)


3 POINTS - MODERATELY INNOVATIVE:
  • Meaningful improvement over existing approaches
    • Innovative within fragrance but not unprecedented
      • Good execution of emerging trend
        • Some novel elements

Examples:
  • Premium refillable bottles (innovative in 2020, standard by 2026)
    • Personalized fragrance quiz (cognitive science-based)
      • Sustainable ingredient sourcing


2 POINTS - INCREMENTAL INNOVATION:
  • Small improvements to existing concepts
    • Following established innovation paths
      • Safe, predictable approach
        • Limited novelty

Examples:
  • Another flanker with different concentration
    • Line extension with new scent notes
      • Packaging redesign without functional innovation


1 POINT - MINIMAL INNOVATION:
  • Barely different from existing products
    • Copying current market offerings
      • No meaningful novelty
        • "Me-too" approach


0 POINTS - NOT INNOVATIVE:
  • Identical to existing products
    • No innovation whatsoever


Innovation Sub-Criteria Breakdown:


A. PRODUCT INNOVATION (Max 2 points):
  • Novel ingredients (1 point: new, 2 points: proprietary like Osmobloom™)
    • Olfactive structure innovation (1 point: unusual, 2 points: unprecedented)
      • Form factor innovation (1 point: new application, 2 points: category-creating)

B. TECH & SCIENCE INNOVATION (Max 2 points):
  • Technology integration (1 point: existing tech new use, 2 points: breakthrough tech)
    • Scientific advancement (1 point: improved process, 2 points: new discovery)
      • Personalization capability (1 point: basic, 2 points: AI-powered individual)

C. EXPERIENCE INNOVATION (Max 1 point):
  • Consumer journey (0.5 point: improved, 1 point: reimagined)
    • Service model (0.5 point: enhanced, 1 point: transformative)

Total Cap: 5 points maximum


CRITERION #2: SUSTAINABLE (5 POINTS)


Official Definition:

"Take responsibility and be mindful of the impact your project will have on the planet"


Scoring Rubric:


5 POINTS - SUSTAINABILITY LEADERSHIP:
  • Multiple sustainability dimensions addressed comprehensively
    • Circular economy model (refillable, recyclable, biodegradable)
      • Regenerative approach (improves environment, not just less harm)
        • Transparent, traceable supply chain
          • Carbon neutral or negative
            • Biodiversity preservation or enhancement
              • Industry-leading certifications achievable

Examples:
  • Osmobloom™ (water-free extraction, flowers left intact, biodiversity preserved)
    • Lancôme Domaine de la Rose (7 hectares organic, 280+ species refuge)
      • Prada Paradoxe refill (saves 44% glass, 67% plastic, 100% metals)

Questions:
  • Does this IMPROVE the environment (not just reduce harm)?
    • Is it circular by design (cradle-to-cradle)?
      • Does it set new industry sustainability standards?
        • Would EcoVadis Platinum recognize this?


4 POINTS - HIGHLY SUSTAINABLE:
  • Strong sustainability across multiple dimensions
    • Refillable infrastructure included
      • Reduced environmental footprint vs. industry standard (30%+ improvement)
        • Sustainable sourcing documented
          • Carbon impact measured and minimized
            • Recyclable packaging

Examples:
  • Multi-brand refill fountain integration
    • La Vie est Belle 15% weight reduction (saves 1M bottles glass/year)
      • 97% renewable energy operations


3 POINTS - MODERATELY SUSTAINABLE:
  • Meaningful sustainability efforts
    • Some refillable or recyclable elements
      • Reduced footprint vs standard (15-30% improvement)
        • Some sustainable sourcing
          • Carbon impact considered

Examples:
  • Refillable bottle without infrastructure
    • Reduced plastic content
      • Some biobased ingredients


2 POINTS - BASIC SUSTAINABILITY:
  • Minimum sustainability standards met
    • Complies with regulations
      • Some recycled content
        • Vague sustainability claims


1 POINT - MINIMAL EFFORT:
  • Token sustainability gestures
    • Greenwashing risk
      • No measurable impact


0 POINTS - NO SUSTAINABILITY:
  • No consideration of environmental impact
    • Harmful materials
      • Disposable by design


Sustainability Sub-Criteria Breakdown:


A. PRODUCT DESIGN (Max 2 points):
  • Refillable/reusable (2 points: full system, 1 point: bottle only, 0 points: disposable)
    • Materials (1 point: recycled/biobased, 0 points: virgin/harmful)
      • Durability (1 point: made to last, 0 points: single-use)

B. INGREDIENTS/FORMULA (Max 1.5 points):
  • Sourcing (1 point: sustainable/traceable, 0.5 point: some sustainable, 0 points: conventional)
    • Biodegradability (0.5 point: biodegradable formula, 0 points: not)

C. OPERATIONS & SUPPLY CHAIN (Max 1.5 points):
  • Carbon footprint (1 point: carbon neutral/negative, 0.5 point: reduced, 0 points: high)
    • Water usage (0.5 point: water-free/recycled, 0 points: high water use)

Total Cap: 5 points maximum


CRITERION #3: INCLUSIVE (5 POINTS)


Official Definition:

"Be mindful of diverse needs in our society to not exclude any particular social groups (gender, sexual orientation, socio-economic and multicultural origins, people with disability, all ages & generations), and make your project as equally accessible as possible to all potential users"


Scoring Rubric:


5 POINTS - EXEMPLARY INCLUSIVITY:
  • Designed FOR diversity from inception (not adapted)
    • Addresses multiple diversity dimensions (4+ of the following):
      • Gender & LGBTQIA+ inclusivity
        • Socio-economic accessibility
          • Multicultural sensitivity
            • Disability accommodation
              • Multi-generational appeal
                • Body type/size inclusivity
                  • Removes barriers others don't address
                    • Could become inclusion benchmark
                      • Co-created WITH diverse communities

Examples:
  • HAPTA (enables 50M people with limited motor skills)
    • YSL Scent-Sation (personalizes for every individual's unique biology)
      • Multi-generational appeal (Gen Z to Gen X different needs met)

Questions:
  • Who might be EXCLUDED by current solutions?
    • How does this actively INCLUDE them?
      • Was it co-created with diverse groups?
        • Does it remove systemic barriers?


4 POINTS - HIGHLY INCLUSIVE:
  • Strong inclusivity across 3 diversity dimensions
    • Meaningful barrier removal
      • Accessible pricing tiers or formats
        • Cultural sensitivity demonstrated
          • Some co-creation with diverse groups

Examples:
  • Fragrance layering system (personal expression across identities)
    • Multi-concentration tiers (different price points)
      • Gender-neutral positioning with diverse ambassadors


3 POINTS - MODERATELY INCLUSIVE:
  • Addresses 2 diversity dimensions
    • Some accessibility considerations
      • Doesn't exclude, but doesn't actively include
        • Standard industry inclusivity

Examples:
  • Gender-neutral scent
    • Multiple size options
      • Diverse marketing imagery


2 POINTS - BASIC INCLUSIVITY:
  • Addresses 1 dimension superficially
    • Minimum compliance with inclusivity expectations
      • May have unintentional barriers


1 POINT - MINIMAL EFFORT:
  • Token diversity in marketing
    • Product design not inclusive
      • Significant barriers remain


0 POINTS - EXCLUSIONARY:
  • Actively excludes groups
    • Discriminatory positioning
      • Accessibility ignored


Inclusivity Sub-Criteria Breakdown:


A. PRODUCT ACCESSIBILITY (Max 2 points):
  • Physical accessibility (1 point: usable by people with disabilities, 0 points: barriers exist)
    • Sensory accessibility (1 point: accounts for sensitivities, 0 points: one-size-fits-all)

B. ECONOMIC ACCESSIBILITY (Max 1.5 points):
  • Price range (1 point: multiple tiers, 0.5 point: mid-range, 0 points: ultra-premium only)
    • Value proposition (0.5 point: refillable/lasting value, 0 points: disposable luxury)

C. CULTURAL & IDENTITY INCLUSIVITY (Max 1.5 points):
  • Gender inclusivity (0.5 point: gender-neutral or multiple options)
    • Cultural sensitivity (0.5 point: respectful of diverse cultures)
      • Representation (0.5 point: co-created with or representative of diverse groups)

Total Cap: 5 points maximum


CRITERION #4: FEASIBLE (5 POINTS)


Official Definition:

"Project must be realistic and possible to implement"


Scoring Rubric:


5 POINTS - HIGHLY FEASIBLE:
  • Technology exists and is accessible
    • Cost structure realistic and profitable
      • Supply chain achievable
        • Regulatory compliance clear path
          • Timeline realistic (2-3 years to market)
            • L'Oréal has necessary capabilities or partnerships
              • Selling price makes sense for target market
                • Business model proven or low-risk

Examples:
  • Extending existing Osmobloom™ to new flowers (tech proven)
    • Expanding multi-brand refill fountain (infrastructure piloted)
      • New concentration of existing successful fragrance

Questions:
  • Does L'Oréal already have this technology/capability?
    • Is the cost structure profitable at proposed price?
      • Can this be manufactured at scale?
        • Are there regulatory blockers?


4 POINTS - FEASIBLE WITH EFFORT:
  • Technology exists but requires adaptation
    • Cost structure needs optimization but achievable
      • Supply chain complex but manageable
        • Regulatory path clear
          • Timeline realistic with focused effort (3-4 years)
            • May require new partnerships
              • Selling price reasonable with scale

Examples:
  • Adapting AI technology from skincare to fragrance personalization
    • New sustainable ingredient requiring new supply chain
      • Connected device requiring IoT infrastructure


3 POINTS - MODERATELY FEASIBLE:
  • Technology emerging but not fully proven
    • Cost structure challenging but possible
      • Supply chain requires significant development
        • Regulatory uncertainty but likely approvable
          • Timeline aggressive (4-5 years)
            • Requires significant new capabilities
              • Selling price requires premium positioning

Examples:
  • Novel extraction technology (not yet scaled)
    • Biotech ingredients (R&D phase)
      • Complex phygital experience (multiple tech integrations)


2 POINTS - CHALLENGING FEASIBILITY:
  • Technology in early stages
    • Cost structure difficult to justify
      • Supply chain major challenge
        • Regulatory approval uncertain
          • Timeline unrealistic for competition timeframe
            • Requires capabilities L'Oréal doesn't have


1 POINT - LOW FEASIBILITY:
  • Technology speculative
    • Cost prohibitive
      • Supply chain unclear
        • Regulatory blockers likely
          • Not achievable in foreseeable future


0 POINTS - NOT FEASIBLE:
  • Impossible with current technology
    • Economically unviable
      • Legally prohibited
        • Contradicts laws of physics/chemistry


Feasibility Sub-Criteria Breakdown:


A. TECHNICAL FEASIBILITY (Max 2 points):
  • Technology readiness (2 points: proven at scale, 1 point: prototype exists, 0 points: concept only)
    • L'Oréal capabilities (1 point: internal capability, 0.5 point: partnership available, 0 points: no path)

B. ECONOMIC FEASIBILITY (Max 2 points):
  • Cost structure (1 point: profitable at proposed price, 0.5 point: needs scale, 0 points: uneconomic)
    • Investment required (1 point: low/medium, 0.5 point: high but justified, 0 points: prohibitive)

C. OPERATIONAL FEASIBILITY (Max 1 point):
  • Supply chain (0.5 point: existing or easy to build, 0 points: major challenge)
    • Regulatory (0.5 point: clear path, 0 points: uncertain/blocked)

Total Cap: 5 points maximum

Official Definition:

"Present a project realizable on a big scale"


Scoring Rubric:


5 POINTS - GLOBALLY SCALABLE:
  • Easily adaptable across all geographies (US, Europe, China, emerging markets)

        • Supply chain can support global demand
          • Regulatory approval achievable in all major markets
            • Business model works at 1M+ units

Examples:
  • YSL Scent-Sation online version (digital = infinite scale)

Questions:
  • Can this work in China, US, Europe, emerging markets?
    • Does scale improve economics (better with volume)?
      • Can infrastructure support 10X growth?
        • Is it franchise-able across L'Oréal's 27 brands?


4 POINTS - HIGHLY SCALABLE:
  • Works in multiple regions (3+ of: NA, Europe, Asia, LATAM, MEA)
    • Minor adaptations needed for different markets
      • Technology/infrastructure scales well
        • Supply chain can support significant growth
          • Regulatory feasible in major markets
            • Business model works at 100K+ units
              • Could apply to multiple brands with adaptation

Examples:
  • Lancôme Cell BioPrint launching China + Japan then global
    • Premium refillable bottles across portfolio
      • In-store personalization experiences


3 POINTS - MODERATELY SCALABLE:
  • Works in 2 major regions
    • Requires meaningful market adaptations
      • Technology scales but with investment
        • Supply chain needs development for scale
          • Regulatory varies by market
            • Business model profitable at 10K+ units
              • Limited to specific brands

Examples:
  • Cultural-specific aesthetic territories
    • Region-specific ingredient sourcing
      • High-touch in-store experiences (limited by physical retail)


2 POINTS - LIMITED SCALABILITY:
  • Works in 1 region only
    • Significant barriers to other markets
      • Technology hard to scale
        • Supply chain constrained
          • Regulatory approval limited geographies
            • Requires high unit economics (low volume)


1 POINT - MINIMAL SCALABILITY:
  • Pilot only
    • Cannot scale beyond test market
      • Prohibitive economics at scale


0 POINTS - NOT SCALABLE:
  • One-off project
    • Cannot be replicated


Scalability Sub-Criteria Breakdown:


A. GEOGRAPHIC SCALABILITY (Max 2 points):
  • Market reach (2 points: global, 1 point: multi-region, 0 points: single market)
    • Cultural adaptability (1 point: universal appeal or easy adaptation, 0 points: culture-specific)

B. OPERATIONAL SCALABILITY (Max 2 points):
  • Production scale (1 point: scales easily, 0.5 point: requires investment, 0 points: constrained)
    • Infrastructure scale (1 point: digital or network effects, 0.5 point: physical but expandable, 0 points: limited)

C. FRANCHISE SCALABILITY (Max 1 point):
  • Brand application (1 point: works across L'Oréal Luxe portfolio, 0.5 point: limited to similar brands, 0 points: single brand only)

SECTION B: TEAM EVALUATION (25 POINTS)


Official Framework from Mission Document:


Teams evaluated on 5 criteria. Each criterion scored: 5 points (Strongly Observed), 2 points (Observed), or 0 points (Not Observed).



CRITERION #1: JUDGMENT (MAX 5 POINTS)


Official Question:

"What guided your decision-making in complex situations?"


Evaluation:


5 POINTS - STRONGLY OBSERVED:
  • Clear decision-making framework articulated
    • Evidence of considering multiple perspectives
      • Data-driven choices balanced with intuition
        • Demonstrates learning from past decisions
          • Strategic thinking (long-term implications considered)
            • Trade-offs explicitly acknowledged and reasoned
              • Could explain "why" behind every major choice

Examples of Strong Judgment:
  • "We chose Osmobloom™ ingredients despite higher cost because proprietary advantage creates dupe immunity worth premium pricing"
    • "We targeted Experts first, then Newcomers (not both simultaneously) based on Exception study showing Expert abandonment when mass adopts"
      • "We planned 5-year re-enchantment despite commercial pressure to focus on launch, knowing lifecycle pattern shows 3-5 year pivot needed"


2 POINTS - OBSERVED:
  • Some decision-making rationale provided
    • Considered couple perspectives
      • Mix of data and gut feeling
        • Some learning from experience
          • Tactical thinking (immediate considerations)

Examples:
  • "We chose refillable packaging because it's sustainable"
    • "We decided on this price point because it's competitive"
      • "We picked this influencer because they're popular"


0 POINTS - NOT OBSERVED:
  • No rationale for decisions
    • Arbitrary choices
      • Ignored evidence
        • No learning demonstrated
          • Decisions contradict each other


Assessment Questions for Judges:


  • Did team explain WHY, not just WHAT they decided?
  • Were trade-offs acknowledged (not just benefits)?
  • Did they consider expert analysis in decision-making?
  • Can they articulate decision-making principles?
  • Did they learn and adjust from initial assumptions?


CRITERION #2: RESILIENCE (MAX 5 POINTS)


Official Question:

"What were the hurdles during the project and how did the team overcome them?"


Evaluation:


5 POINTS - STRONGLY OBSERVED:
  • Specific hurdles identified and overcome
    • Team shows adaptability and persistence
      • Creative problem-solving demonstrated
        • Turned obstacles into opportunities
          • Maintained morale and momentum
            • Learned and grew stronger from challenges
              • Evidence of iteration and improvement

Examples of Strong Resilience:
  • "Initial juice testing showed head notes copied easily by dupes. We pivoted to emphasize complex drydown architecture, turning weakness into competitive advantage"
    • "When cost analysis showed our tech infrastructure was too expensive, we redesigned for digital-first with physical overlay, reducing costs 60% while improving scalability"
      • "Our first narrative was marketing fiction. After Expert feedback, we rebuilt around authentic brand heritage, which was harder but created defensible positioning"


2 POINTS - OBSERVED:
  • Some challenges mentioned
    • Basic problem-solving
      • Kept going despite difficulties
        • Some adaptation

Examples:
  • "We faced timeline challenges but worked extra hours"
    • "Initial idea didn't work so we tried another approach"
      • "Team had disagreements but we resolved them"


0 POINTS - NOT OBSERVED:
  • No challenges mentioned (unrealistic)
    • Gave up when faced with obstacles
      • Blamed external factors
        • No adaptation or learning


Assessment Questions for Judges:


  • Were real, meaningful obstacles overcome (not trivial)?
  • Did team show creativity in problem-solving?
  • Did challenges improve the final project?
  • Was there evidence of iteration based on feedback?
  • Did team maintain positive attitude through difficulties?

5 POINTS - STRONGLY OBSERVED:

  • Is there a 5-10 year roadmap?

  • Is ambition balanced with feasibility?
  • Do they articulate impact beyond sales?

TIER 2: L'ORÉAL SUCCESS PATTERN ALIGNMENT


Strategic Assessment Beyond Official Criteria (100 POINTS)


This tier evaluates alignment with L'Oréal's proven success patterns identified in our deep analysis. While not official competition criteria, these predict real-world market success.



DIMENSION 1: FOUNDATIONAL VALUES ALIGNMENT (20 POINTS)


Evaluates how well innovation embodies L'Oréal Luxe's 5 core values.


A. CULTURE DE L'ÉCART - THE TWIST (5 points)


5 points: Embodies "twist culture" perfectly - seeks what's never been done, pushes boundaries, creates the "écart" (gap) between L'Oréal and competition

3 points: Shows some twist/disruption but not fundamental reimagination

1 point: Incremental, no real "écart"

0 points: Follows conventional path


Assessment Question: Does this make you say "Only L'Oréal Luxe could do this"?



B. ART & SCIENCE FUSION (4 points)


4 points: Masterful balance - artistic vision + scientific rigor, neither dominates

2 points: Leans art OR science, other dimension weak

0 points: Art alone (beautiful but unscalable) OR science alone (functional but soulless)


Assessment Question: Would this impress both perfumers (art) AND scientists (tech)?



C. INCLUSIVITY & ACCESSIBILITY (4 points)


4 points: Designed FOR diversity, removes barriers others don't address

2 points: Doesn't exclude but doesn't actively include

0 points: Exclusive or ignores accessibility


Assessment Question: Who is STILL excluded by current solutions that this includes?



D. SUSTAINABILITY & RESPONSIBILITY (4 points)


4 points: Regenerative (improves environment), circular economy model

2 points: Reduced harm, recyclable elements

0 points: Standard disposable model


Assessment Question: Would CDP Triple A and EcoVadis Platinum recognize this?



E. FRENCH HERITAGE + GLOBAL REACH (3 points)


3 points: Authentically French (craft, perfume heritage) yet globally scalable

2 points: Generic luxury OR culturally specific without scale

1 point: Neither authentically rooted nor scalable

    • Complex olfactive architecture?
    • Brand legitimacy?
    • Premium craftsmanship?
    • Personalized experience?
    • Connected ecosystem?
    • What literally CAN'T dupes copy? (Osmobloom™, AI infrastructure, heritage)

    • What takes time for dupes to copy? (Brand building, expert validation)

    Q5: HOW DOES TECH ENHANCE (NOT REPLACE)?

    Must answer:

    • What is the human artistry? (Perfumer vision, artisan craft, personal ritual)
    • What does tech augment? (Capabilities, personalization, scale, discovery)
    • Is tech "invisible magic"? (Consumer experiences wonder, not machinery)
    • Does tech enable proprietary advantage? (Osmobloom™, Scent-Sation, Cell BioPrint)

    Q6: WHAT IS THE TIME HORIZON?

    Must answer:

    • What is Year 1 goal? (Expert discovery, niche launch)
    • What is Year 3 goal? (Mainstream awareness, bestseller status)
    • What is Year 5 goal? (Icon status OR re-enchantment)
    • How do you avoid "shooting star"? (Foundation before virality)
    • When does re-enchantment happen? (Plan now, execute year 3-5)

    Reality Check: If planning for immediate viral success → RISKY. Plan for 3-7 year build.



    Q7: WHAT IS THE CULTURE DE L'ÉCART TWIST?

    Must answer:

    • What boundary are you pushing? (Convention being broken)
    • What is the "écart" (gap)? (Between L'Oréal and everyone else)

    • Why can only L'Oréal do this? (Proprietary advantage, unique position)

    PLAYBOOK A: CRAFTING THE EXCEPTIONAL JUICE


    The non-negotiable foundation. If juice is mediocre, nothing else matters.


    Step 1: Perfumer Brief Development


    Bad Brief Example:

    "Create a fresh floral fragrance for young women that's modern and elegant."

    • Too generic

          • No Culture de l'Écart

    Good Brief Example:

    "Create a fragrance capturing the olfactive moment when lavender fields at Lancôme's Domaine de la Rose encounter first morning frost—the contrast between warm herbal lavender and icy metallic ozone. Budget: Premium ingredient spend approved. Timeline: Perfumer has creative freedom, not rushed."


    Why It Works:
    • Specific olfactive direction ("warm cold")
      • Authentic inspiration (real place, real moment)
        • Built-in proprietary advantage (Osmobloom™)
          • Targets right audience first (Experts)
            • Respects perfumer artistry (not committee-designed)


    Step 2: Olfactive Incident Design


    Remember: All 12 Exception successes have olfactive incident—ingredient in overdose or unusual combination creating signature.


    Techniques:

    Technique 1: OVERDOSE
    • Choose one note, use 10X normal concentration
      • Example: Baccarat Rouge 540 (saffron overdose)
        • Risk: Polarizing (accept this - Experts appreciate boldness)

    Technique 2: OXYMORON
    • Combine opposites that shouldn't work
      • Example: "Fresh tobacco," "sweet oud," "cold jasmine"
        • Risk: Confusion (test extensively)

    Technique 3: DECONSTRUCTION
    • Use raw ingredient close to pure accord
      • Example: Santal 33 (sandalwood nearly pure)
        • Risk: "Unfinished" perception (educate on intentionality)

    Technique 4: UNEXPECTED HERO
    • Make background note the star
      • Example: Molecule 01 (Iso E Super alone, typically just base)
        • Risk: Too minimalist (not for everyone)

    Technique 5: CULTURAL REMIX
    • Take ingredient from one culture, structure from another
      • Example: Oud (Middle Eastern) in French perfumery structure
        • Risk: Cultural appropriation (must be respectful, authentic)


    Step 3: Proprietary Ingredient Sourcing


    Goal: Use something dupes CANNOT access.


    Option A: Osmobloom™ (Recommended if available)
    • L'Oréal exclusive extraction technology
      • Can extract "mute" flowers
        • Water-free, biodiversity-preserving
          • Literally impossible for dupes to copy
            • How to use: Partner with L'Oréal R&D, request access for Brandstorm project

    Option B: Exclusive Origin Story
    • Source ingredient from specific location with story
      • Example: "Roses harvested only at dawn from Lancôme's Domaine de la Rose"
        • Dupes can copy rose, but NOT the origin story + brand connection
          • How to use: Research L'Oréal's owned/partnered sources

    Option C: Biotech Innovation
    • Lab-grown molecules (sustainable + unique)
      • Example: Lab-created musk (animal-free, consistent)
        • Expensive for dupes to replicate
          • How to use: Research fragrance biotech companies, propose partnership

    Option D: Upcycled Ingredients
    • Extract scent from waste materials (coffee grounds, wine by-products)
      • Sustainability + uniqueness combined
        • How to use: Circular economy approach, partner with food/beverage industries


    Step 4: Complex Drydown Architecture


    Remember: Dupes copy head notes easily, struggle with drydown complexity.


    Design for Revelation:
    • 0-15 minutes (TOP NOTES): Initial impression, can be simple/recognizable
      • 15 minutes - 2 hours (HEART NOTES): Where magic starts, unique accords
        • 2-8+ hours (BASE NOTES): The moat, complex interactions only on skin

    Strategic Drydown:
    • Accept dupes will copy top notes (don't fight this battle)
      • INVEST in base notes (this is your competitive advantage)
        • Use skin chemistry interaction (changes per person = personalized)
          • Educate consumers: "The real fragrance reveals itself after 2 hours"

    Drydown Complexity Techniques:
    • Multiple base notes that interact (not linear)
      • Temperature-reactive molecules (change with body heat)
        • pH-reactive molecules (change with skin acidity)
          • Time-release encapsulation (staged revelation)

      • AI personalizes (tech adds value)
        • Consumer gets both expertise AND personalization (best of both)

    Use Case 2: Olfactory Memory Unlocking
    • Scent triggers memory centers (limbic system)

    Use Case 3: Mood-Scent Correlation
    • Track mood when consumer wears different fragrances

        • Displays personalized info (when opened, shows "Your Perfect Layering Today: ___")

    2. EMBRACE CULTURE DE L'ÉCART (THE TWIST)


    L'Oréal WANTS bold, boundary-pushing, unprecedented.

    • "A new fragrance with sustainable packaging" (done)

        • Something that makes them say "This changes the game"

    Test: If your innovation feels safe → PUSH FURTHER. The twist is your edge.



    3. OBSESS OVER THE JUICE (IT'S NON-NEGOTIABLE)


    No amount of tech, experience, or marketing can save mediocre olfaction.


    Remember:
    • 30% of Exception conversation = scent profiles
      • 29% of virality = scent profiles
        • Experts validate based on olfactive quality FIRST

    If your pitch says "The fragrance smells [generic description]" → RED FLAG.


    It should say: "The fragrance uses [specific proprietary ingredient] in [unprecedented way] creating [olfactive incident] that reveals [complex drydown] over [timeline], evoking [specific emotion] through [authentic story]."



    4. RESPECT TIME (IT'S STRATEGIC, NOT A CONSTRAINT)


    Exception successes take 3-7 years. Shooting stars fail.


    Don't pitch:
    • "This will go viral immediately and be a bestseller in Year 1"
      • → Judges know this is naive

    Do pitch:
    • "Year 1: Expert discovery in 10 exclusive boutiques. Year 3: Mainstream awareness after organic validation. Year 5: Icon status with re-enchantment strategy launching Limited Artist Collaboration series."
      • → Judges see you understand lifecycle

    Strategic patience = competitive advantage (dupes chase quick hits, you build endurance)



    5. LAYER UNDUPABILITY (IT'S YOUR MOAT)


    Success attracts dupes. Build immunity from day one.


    Minimum: 4 of 6 layers (score 18+/25)

    Recommended: 5 of 6 layers (score 20+/25)

    Exceptional: All 6 layers (score 23+/25)


    Remember: Each layer multiplies defense:

    • 1 layer = dupes copy in 6 months
    • 3 layers = dupes copy in 18 months
    • 6 layers = dupes give up or produce inferior version


    6. BALANCE ART & SCIENCE (DON'T PICK SIDES)


    L'Oréal's DNA is the fusion.


    Avoid:
    • All art, no science (beautiful but unscalable)
      • All science, no art (functional but soulless)

    Aim for:
    • Perfumer's artistic vision (human creativity)
      • × Proprietary tech (Osmobloom™, AI, neuroscience)
        • × Scientific rigor (testing, validation, optimization)
          • = Innovation only L'Oréal can deliver

    Test: Would both perfumers AND scientists respect your innovation? If only one → rebalance.



    7. SERVE DUAL TARGET SEQUENTIALLY (NOT SIMULTANEOUSLY)


    Biggest mistake: Trying to appeal to Experts and Newcomers from launch.


    Right Approach:
    • Year 0-2: Expert-first (confidentiality, technique, discovery)
      • Year 2-3: Newcomer activation (virality, status, scale)
        • Year 3-5: Maintain both (tiered offerings, re-enchantment)

    Why: Experts abandon if Newcomers adopt too fast. Sequential preserves both.



    8. MAKE IT REAL (PROTOTYPES MATTER)


    Concepts are cheap. Tangibility is convincing.


    Invest time in:
    • High-quality renders (bottle, packaging, retail)
      • User journey visualization (storyboard, video)
        • Experience mockup (how it feels to use)

    Judges need to PICTURE it. The team that makes judging easiest (visual > abstract) wins.



    9. OWN YOUR FEASIBILITY (NO HAND-WAVING)


    Saying "We'd partner with tech company" = WEAK.


    Better:
    • "We'd partner with [Specific Company] who has [proven capability] in [relevant tech]. We've researched their [case study/precedent]. Integration would take [realistic timeline] and cost approximately [researched estimate]."

    Know your numbers:
    • What's the COGS (Cost of Goods Sold)?
      • What's the selling price?
        • What's the margin?
          • What's the break-even volume?
            • What's the Year 5 revenue projection?

    Even rough estimates > vague "it's profitable."



    10. TELL A STORY (NOT A FEATURE LIST)


    Humans remember stories, forget facts.

    • "Our innovation has AI, refillable packaging, Osmobloom™ ingredient, and omnichannel experience."
      • → Laundry list, no narrative

    • "Meet Sophia, a fragrance collector who's tried everything. She discovers [Your Innovation] at a hidden Parisian boutique. This is the future of luxury fragrance."
      • → Journey, emotion, tangible

    Make judges FEEL it, not just understand it.
